% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
Selective state space models (SSMs) such as Mamba achieve strong performance through input-dependent dynamics ($\Delta$, $\mathbf{B}$, $\mathbf{C}$ derived from input), but this selectivity creates \emph{multiplicative noise propagation}: under low SNR, noise corrupts the selection parameters themselves, causing $\Delta(x) \cdot B(x) \cdot x \propto \text{noise}^3$.
In contrast, CNNs use fixed filters where noise propagates only \emph{additively} ($W \cdot x$), explaining their structural advantage under noise augmentation.
We propose \textbf{SM-SSM} (Selectivity-Modulated SSM), which smoothly interpolates between selective (Mamba) and fixed (LTI) dynamics via an SNR-derived gate $\sigma$:
at high SNR, $\sigma{\approx}1$ preserves full Mamba adaptivity;
at low SNR, $\sigma{\approx}0$ transitions to LTI-SSM---mathematically equivalent to a \emph{learned causal convolution}---providing CNN-like noise immunity.
We prove that SM-SSM's hypothesis space strictly contains standard SSM's (superset guarantee), and that output variance under noise reduces from $O(\sigma_n^6)$ to $O(\sigma_n^2)$.
Applied to NanoMamba for keyword spotting, SM-SSM adds only \textbf{+38~parameters} (0.51\%) to achieve this structural unification, with 7,440~total parameters---fewer than BC-ResNet-1 (7,464).
% Experimental results TBD after training.
\end{abstract}

% 1. INTRODUCTION
% ============================================================
\section{Introduction}

Keyword spotting (KWS) on edge devices requires models under sub-256\,KB with sub-10\,ms inference~\cite{banbury2021mlperf,lin2020mcunet}.
Selective state space models (SSMs)~\cite{gu2024mamba,gu2022efficiently} offer linear-time sequential processing with constant memory, making them ideal for edge deployment.
Recent SSM-based KWS models~\cite{goel2024keyword} show promising accuracy, but a persistent gap remains: \emph{under noisy conditions, CNN architectures consistently outperform SSMs of comparable size}.

For example, BC-ResNet-1~\cite{kim2021bcresnet} (7,464 params) outperforms NanoMamba (7,402 params) under noise augmentation, despite using nearly identical parameter budgets.
Prior attempts to close this gap---adding frequency-axis SSM scanning, learnable spectral enhancement---failed to improve noise robustness, suggesting the problem is \emph{structural}, not a matter of model capacity or feature engineering.

In this work, we identify the root cause: \textbf{multiplicative noise propagation} inherent to selective SSMs.
Unlike CNNs, where fixed kernels $W$ create additive noise ($W{\cdot}n$), selective SSMs derive their dynamics ($\Delta$, $\mathbf{B}$, $\mathbf{C}$) \emph{from the input itself}.
Under noise, the input corrupts these dynamics, and the resulting state update $\Delta(x){\cdot}B(x){\cdot}x$ creates a \emph{cubic} noise coupling---a fundamental structural disadvantage that no amount of SNR-based scaling can fully eliminate.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
\setlength\itemsep{0pt}
\item \textbf{Noise propagation analysis}: We formally characterize the multiplicative ($O(\sigma_n^6)$) vs.\ additive ($O(\sigma_n^2)$) noise variance gap between selective SSMs and CNNs (Section~\ref{sec:analysis}).
\item \textbf{SM-SSM}: A selectivity modulation mechanism that smoothly interpolates between selective (Mamba) and fixed (LTI $\approx$ learned causal convolution) dynamics based on SNR, adding only $3N{+}4$ parameters per block (Section~\ref{sec:smssm}).
\item \textbf{Theoretical guarantees}: SM-SSM's hypothesis space strictly contains standard SSM's, ensuring no degradation; output variance at low SNR reduces from $O(\sigma_n^6)$ to $O(\sigma_n^2)$ (Section~\ref{sec:theory}).
\end{itemize}

% 2. BACKGROUND

\section{Background: Selective SSM and the Noise Gap}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Selective State Space Models}

A selective SSM~\cite{gu2024mamba} processes input $x_t \in \mathbb{R}^D$ with \emph{input-dependent} parameters:
\begin{align}
[\Delta_t, \mathbf{B}_t, \mathbf{C}_t] &= W_{\text{proj}} \cdot x_t, \label{eq:proj} \\
\mathbf{h}_t &= \bar{\mathbf{A}}_t \mathbf{h}_{t-1} + \bar{\mathbf{B}}_t x_t, \quad
y_t = \mathbf{C}_t \mathbf{h}_t + \mathbf{D} x_t, \label{eq:ssm}
\end{align}
where $\bar{\mathbf{A}}_t = \exp(\mathbf{A} \cdot \Delta_t)$, $\bar{\mathbf{B}}_t = \Delta_t \cdot \mathbf{B}_t$.
This input-dependent selectivity enables Mamba to modulate what information to retain or discard at each step---the key to its strong performance on clean data.

\subsection{CNN: Fixed Filters and Additive Noise}

A CNN convolves input with \emph{fixed, learned} kernels $W$:
\begin{equation}
y = W \ast x = W \ast (s + n) = \underbrace{W \ast s}_{\text{signal}} + \underbrace{W \ast n}_{\text{noise (additive)}}.
\label{eq:cnn}
\end{equation}
Since $W$ is independent of the input, noise enters the output only through a single linear transformation. Under noise augmentation, $W$ learns to suppress noise linearly---a \emph{structurally stable} behavior.

% ============================================================
% 3. ANALYSIS: MULTIPLICATIVE VS ADDITIVE NOISE
% ============================================================
\section{Analysis: Why Selective SSMs Fail Under Noise}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{Multiplicative Noise in Selective SSM}

In Eq.~\ref{eq:ssm}, the input contribution to the state update is:
\begin{equation}
u_t = \underbrace{\Delta_t}_{\text{from } x} \cdot \underbrace{\mathbf{B}_t}_{\text{from } x} \cdot \underbrace{x_t}_{\text{input}} = f_\Delta(x_t) \cdot f_B(x_t) \cdot x_t.
\label{eq:mult}
\end{equation}
All three factors depend on $x_t$. When $x_t = s_t + n_t$ (signal + noise):
\begin{equation}
u_t = \big(f_\Delta(s{+}n)\big) \big(f_B(s{+}n)\big) (s{+}n),
\end{equation}
which, since $f_\Delta$ and $f_B$ are linear projections, expands to include \emph{cubic cross-terms} in $n$:
\begin{equation}
u_t = \underbrace{f_\Delta(s) f_B(s) s}_{\text{clean}} + \underbrace{f_\Delta(n) f_B(n) n}_{\text{noise}^3} + \text{mixed terms}.
\label{eq:cubic}
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}[Noise variance comparison]
\label{prop:variance}
Let $n \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_n^2 I)$. The variance of the state update input contribution satisfies:
\begin{align}
\textbf{Selective SSM:} \quad \mathrm{Var}[u_t] &= O(\sigma_n^6), \label{eq:var_ssm} \\
\textbf{CNN / LTI-SSM:} \quad \mathrm{Var}[u_t] &= O(\sigma_n^2). \label{eq:var_cnn}
\end{align}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
For the selective SSM, each of the three factors in Eq.~\ref{eq:mult} contributes $O(\sigma_n^2)$ variance (linear projections of $n$). Since they are multiplied, the dominant term has variance $O(\sigma_n^{2 \times 3}) = O(\sigma_n^6)$.
For CNN/LTI-SSM with fixed parameters $\theta_\Delta$, $\theta_B$: $u_t = \theta_\Delta \cdot \theta_B \cdot x_t$, where only $x_t$ depends on $n$, giving $\mathrm{Var}[u_t] = \theta_\Delta^2 \lVert\bm{\theta}_B\rVert^2 \sigma_n^2 = O(\sigma_n^2)$.
\end{proof}

\smallskip
At $-15$\,dB ($\sigma_n \approx 5.6$), the ratio is $\sigma_n^6 / \sigma_n^2 = \sigma_n^4 \approx 1{,}000$---selective SSM output variance is \emph{three orders of magnitude} higher than LTI.

\subsection{LTI-SSM as Learned Causal Convolution}
\label{sec:lti}

An LTI (Linear Time-Invariant) SSM uses \emph{fixed} $\bar{A}$, $\bar{B}$, $C$:
\begin{equation}
h_t = \bar{A} h_{t-1} + \bar{B} x_t, \quad y_t = C h_t + D x_t.
\end{equation}
Unrolling the recurrence yields:
\begin{equation}
y_t = \sum_{k=0}^{t} \underbrace{C \bar{A}^{t-k} \bar{B}}_{K_{t-k}} x_k + D x_t = (K \ast x)_t + D x_t,
\label{eq:conv}
\end{equation}
where $K_\tau = C \bar{A}^\tau \bar{B}$ is a fixed, exponentially decaying kernel---a \emph{causal IIR (infinite impulse response) filter}.
This is mathematically equivalent to a learned 1D convolution with infinite (but decaying) receptive field:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\text{LTI-SSM} \equiv \text{Causal IIR Convolution} \equiv \text{Learned Conv1D}.}
\label{eq:equivalence}
\end{equation}
Thus, transitioning from selective to LTI dynamics at low SNR is equivalent to replacing input-dependent dynamics with a \emph{fixed learned convolution}---achieving the same structural noise immunity as CNNs.

\section{Proposed Method: SM-SSM}
\label{sec:smssm}

\subsection{Selectivity Modulation}

SM-SSM introduces an SNR-adaptive gate $\sigma$ that blends selective (input-dependent) and fixed (LTI) dynamics.
Three enhancements address known limitations of naive selectivity modulation:

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{(i) Temporal $\sigma$ smoothing.}
Raw per-frame SNR estimates are noisy, causing gate instability.
We apply a causal 3-frame average to the mean SNR before computing $\sigma$:
$\tilde{s}_t = \frac{1}{3}\sum_{k=0}^{2} \bar{s}_{t-k}$, where $\bar{s}_t = \text{mean}_f\big(s_f/(s_f{+}K)\big)$ uses Michaelis-Menten re-normalization ($K{=}0.05$).

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{(ii) Per-state selectivity.}
Rather than a single gate for all SSM states, $\Delta$ and $(\mathbf{B}, \mathbf{C})$ use separate learnable biases, allowing per-state thresholds:
\begin{align}
\sigma^\Delta_t &= \text{sigmoid}(s_{\text{scale}} \cdot \tilde{s}_t + b_\Delta), \label{eq:sigma_dt} \\
\sigma^{BC}_{t,n} &= \text{sigmoid}(s_{\text{scale}} \cdot \tilde{s}_t + b_{BC,n}), \label{eq:sigma_bc}
\end{align}
where $b_\Delta \in \mathbb{R}$ and $\mathbf{b}_{BC} \in \mathbb{R}^N$.
This enables some states to maintain selectivity for temporal pattern capture while others transition to LTI for noise immunity.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{(iii) PCEN-conditioned $\sigma$.}
Non-stationary noise (babble, music) is harder to handle than stationary noise.
When the DualPCEN gate $g_t$ indicates non-stationarity:
$\sigma \leftarrow \sigma \cdot (1 - \lambda_g \cdot g_t)$,
where $\lambda_g$ is a learnable modulation coefficient ($\lambda_g{=}0.3$ init).

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Blending:}
\begin{align}
\Delta_t &= \sigma^\Delta_t \cdot \Delta_{\text{sel}}(x_t) + (1{-}\sigma^\Delta_t) \cdot \theta_\Delta, \label{eq:blend_dt} \\
\mathbf{B}_t &= \bm{\sigma}^{BC}_t \odot \mathbf{B}_{\text{sel}}(x_t) + (1{-}\bm{\sigma}^{BC}_t) \odot \bm{\theta}_B, \label{eq:blend_b} \\
\mathbf{C}_t &= \bm{\sigma}^{BC}_t \odot \mathbf{C}_{\text{sel}}(x_t) + (1{-}\bm{\sigma}^{BC}_t) \odot \bm{\theta}_C, \label{eq:blend_c}
\end{align}
where $\theta_\Delta \in \mathbb{R}$, $\bm{\theta}_B, \bm{\theta}_C \in \mathbb{R}^N$ are learned fixed (LTI) parameters.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Two extremes:}
\begin{itemize}
\setlength\itemsep{0pt}
\item $\sigma \approx 1$ (high SNR): $\Delta_t \approx \Delta_{\text{sel}}(x_t)$ $\Rightarrow$ \textbf{selective SSM} (full Mamba).
\item $\sigma \approx 0$ (low SNR): $\Delta_t \approx \theta_\Delta$ $\Rightarrow$ \textbf{LTI-SSM} $\equiv$ learned convolution (Eq.~\ref{eq:equivalence}).
\end{itemize}

\noindent This realizes a \emph{universal principle}: selectivity should be inversely proportional to noise level.

\subsection{Theoretical Guarantees}
\label{sec:theory}

\begin{proposition}[Superset guarantee]
\label{prop:superset}
Let $\mathcal{H}_{\text{SSM}}$ denote the hypothesis space of the standard selective SSM, and $\mathcal{H}_{\text{SM}}$ that of SM-SSM. Then $\mathcal{H}_{\text{SM}} \supset \mathcal{H}_{\text{SSM}}$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Setting $\sigma{=}1$ identically recovers $\Delta_t = \Delta_{\text{sel}}(x_t)$, $\mathbf{B}_t = \mathbf{B}_{\text{sel}}(x_t)$, $\mathbf{C}_t = \mathbf{C}_{\text{sel}}(x_t)$---the standard selective SSM.
Since SM-SSM additionally includes all $\sigma{<}1$ configurations, $\mathcal{H}_{\text{SM}} \supset \mathcal{H}_{\text{SSM}}$.
\end{proof}

This ensures SM-SSM's optimal loss $\mathcal{L}^*_{\text{SM}} \leq \mathcal{L}^*_{\text{SSM}}$: it can only improve, never degrade.
The initialization ($\theta_\Delta{=}0$, $\bm{\theta}_B{=}\mathbf{0}$, $s_{\text{bias}}{=}{-1}$ yielding $\sigma{\approx}0.97$ at clean SNR) further ensures training starts from a point functionally identical to the standard SSM.

\begin{proposition}[Variance reduction at low SNR]
\label{prop:var_reduction}
At low SNR where $\sigma \approx 0$, SM-SSM reduces state update variance from $O(\sigma_n^6)$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:var_ssm}) to $O(\sigma_n^2)$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:var_cnn}).
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
At $\sigma{=}0$: $\Delta_t = \theta_\Delta$, $\mathbf{B}_t = \bm{\theta}_B$ (fixed). Thus $u_t = \theta_\Delta \cdot \bm{\theta}_B \cdot x_t$, where only $x_t$ depends on noise. This matches the LTI/CNN case in Proposition~\ref{prop:variance}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Architecture: NanoMamba with SM-SSM}
\label{sec:arch}

SM-SSM is integrated into NanoMamba~(Figure~\ref{fig:arch}), a compact SSM architecture for KWS:

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Frontend: DualPCEN-v2.}
Two parallel PCEN~\cite{wang2017trainable} experts (speech-optimized $\delta{=}0.8$, noise-optimized $\delta{=}0.01$) with SNR-conditioned routing via spectral flatness, spectral tilt, and temporal modulation index (TMI).
The routing gate $g_t \in [0,1]$ doubles as a noise-stationarity estimate for downstream conditioning.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{SNR Estimator.}
Per-band SNR is estimated from the first $K{=}5$ STFT frames as noise floor reference: $\hat{s}_{f,t} = \tanh(|X_{f,t}| / (\gamma \bar{n}_f {+} \epsilon))$, with learnable $\gamma$.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{SM-SSM blocks.}
Each block consists of: LayerNorm $\to$ linear in-projection $\to$ depthwise Conv1d $\to$ SiLU gate $\to$ \textbf{SM-SSM} $\to$ out-projection.
SM-SSM receives per-band SNR $\hat{\mathbf{s}}_t$ and PCEN gate $g_t$ as side information.
Beyond selectivity modulation (Eqs.~\ref{eq:blend_dt}--\ref{eq:blend_c}), SA-SSM mechanisms are retained: $\Delta$-shift from SNR, $\mathbf{B}$-gating, adaptive $\epsilon$-floor, and PCEN gate conditioning for noise-type-aware temporal dynamics.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Parameter overhead.}
SM-SSM adds $3N{+}4$ parameters per block: $\theta_\Delta$: 1, $\bm{\theta}_B$: $N$, $\bm{\theta}_C$: $N$, $s_{\text{scale}}$: 1, $b_\Delta$: 1, $\mathbf{b}_{BC}$: $N$, $\lambda_g$: 1.
Temporal smoothing adds 0 parameters (causal avg pool).
With $N{=}5$ and 2 blocks: \textbf{+38 parameters total (0.51\%)}.

\subsection{Training Enhancements}

Two training techniques complement SM-SSM (applicable to all models):

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{SpecAugment}~\cite{park2019specaugment}: frequency and time masking on mel-spectrograms during training (0 extra params).

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{EMA} (Exponential Moving Average): shadow weights with decay 0.999, applied at validation/test for smoother generalization.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Noise curriculum v2}: type-staged noise introduction (stationary first, then non-stationary) with SNR annealing, enabling progressive learning of noise-robust dynamics.

\subsection{Setup}

\section{Conclusion}

We identified \emph{multiplicative noise propagation} as the root cause of the selective SSM--CNN noise robustness gap: input-dependent dynamics ($\Delta$, $\mathbf{B}$, $\mathbf{C}$ derived from $x$) create $O(\sigma_n^6)$ output variance, versus $O(\sigma_n^2)$ for CNN's fixed filters.

We proposed \textbf{SM-SSM} (Selectivity-Modulated SSM), which interpolates between selective (Mamba) and fixed (LTI $\equiv$ learned causal convolution) dynamics via an SNR-derived gate, providing two theoretical guarantees: (1)~the hypothesis space strictly contains standard SSM (superset), and (2)~noise variance reduces from $O(\sigma_n^6)$ to $O(\sigma_n^2)$ at low SNR.

This realizes a universal principle: \emph{selectivity should be inversely proportional to noise level}.
At high SNR, full Mamba selectivity maximizes adaptivity; at low SNR, LTI dynamics provide CNN-equivalent noise immunity.
SM-SSM achieves this structural unification with only \textbf{+38 parameters (0.51\%)} over the baseline, yielding 7,440 total parameters---fewer than BC-ResNet-1 (7,464).
